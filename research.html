Hadronic and Nuclear Stucture

Nuclear matter is anything made up of protons and neutrons. During the 20th century, it became readily apparent that our picture of these nucleons as fundamental particles is inaccurate. Indeed, the model of hadronic matter, encompassing nuclear matter, being composed of quarks and gluons arose from the work of Gell-Mann and Feynman and was confirmed by the original deep inelastic scattering (DIS) experiments at the Stanford Linear Accelerator (SLAC). Because the nucleons are not fundamental objects, their properties are not just empirically observed facts in the Standard Model. Instead, the global properties of hadrons emerge from their constituent partonic structure. This line of reasoning then opens the door to a whole slew of phenomenological questions. Essentially, the broad question is simply this: how do we reconcile the seemingly inherent properties of nucleons and hadron A few open problems that I have been exposed to and found intriguing are discussed below.

Collinear Factorization and Parton Distribution Functions

For the phenomenological studies being done at high energy colliders and accelerators, the strong coupling becomes small enough such that QCD is perturbative. At these scales, so-called factorization theorems have been derived, which allows the calculation of the hadronic cross section to be broken down into a convolution between the partonic hard scattering, partonic cross section and the appropriate quantum correlation functions (QCFs). For example, in inclusive DIS (\(\ell N \rightarrow \ell' X \)), where \(X\) represents the unobserved hadronic final state particles, we can calculate the hadronic cross section as $$ \frac{ d\sigma}{ dx \, dQ^2 } = \sum_{i} \int_{\xi_{\min}}^{1} \frac{d\xi}{\xi} \frac{ d\hat{\sigma}_i }{ d\hat{x} \, dQ^2} f_{i}(\xi,Q^2), $$ where \(f\) is the unpolarized parton distribution function (PDF), which are (in non-interacting physics) number densities on the momentum fraction \(\xi\) of parton \(i\) inside a hadron. A semi-classical interpretation of this factorization is then as follows: the total cross section is just a weighted sum of direct scattering events from quarks inside a given hadron, where the weights are the PDFs.

The cross sections are not generally interesting: it is the PDFs and other QCFs that contain the physics. But these QCFs are generally nonperturbative. These factorization theorems are then interesting because we can match these PDFs to experimental data from scattering experiments, which then allows us to extract the physics from the PDFs. Therefore, the development of these factorization theorems is interesting because of their phenomenological utility. Additionally, the results generated from factorization theorems serves as a good benchmark for lattice calculations, which solve QCD problems at low energies.

Accessing gluon polarization with high-\(P_T\) hadrons in SIDIS

This project is related to the section above on collinear factorization and PDFs. We focused on the semi-inclusive DIS process \( \ell N \rightarrow \ell' h X \), and its ability to constrain the gluon helicity dependent PDF, which is directly related to the gluon spin contribution to the proton. This is of particular interest in the scope of the proton spin puzzle, which aims to understand how the angular momentum of the proton is distributed between the spin and orbital angular momentum of its quarks and gluons.

In particular, here are a few of the questions we attempted to answer in our study:

  • How sensitive is this process to the gluon channel?
  • Can the JLab accelerator or future Electron-Ion Collider produce kinematics where the double spin asymmetry is sizable enough to be measured
  • Does the double spin asymmetry provide a better ability than other scattering experiments to constrain the gluon helicity PDF?
  • How do the uncertainties look for this observable (both statistical and those from uncertainties in the gluon helicity PDF)?

Developing perfectly matched layer method for heat-diffusion equation

When solving a particular partial differential equation, the unique solution is determined by the imposition of boundary conditions. This means that it is critical to impose the correct boundary conditions such that a physical situation is properly modeled. In particular, for this project we were interested in looking at the boundary conditions allowing heat to freely propagate at the boundaries of the medium. Analytically, this is simple to accomodate because we can consider domains that are infinite, but numerically, we are constrained to work on a finite domain. This leads to the question of how to impose boundary conditions to emulate free boundaries, which allow heat to propagate infinitely from the region of interest.

There are solutions to this problem for the propagation of electromagnetic (EM) waves which utilize "perfectly matched layers". That is, we extend the domain such that the EM waves are damped beyond the physical domain of interest. At the time of this writing, there is no widely known or accepted perfectly matched layer method for the heat-diffusion equation that implements free boundaries. Complications for this differential equation arise primarily because it is first-order in time and second-order in space. The development of an effective numerical approach to solving this problem is of great interest in fields such as biological physics.

Survey of projectile motion under gravitational, drag, and magnus forces

There is a common claim made by baseball players that good curveballs seem to break suddenly in flight. For this project, we implemented a simple differential equation solver to solve the classical equation of motion, accounting for the typical forces on projectiles of gravity and air resistance along with the force produced from the spin of the baseball in a fluid (otherwise known as the magnus force). From this, we determined that the trajectory of a pitch is smooth, as expected. That is, there are no kinks in (discontinuities in the derivative of) the acceleration of the baseball. To naively imitate the sight of a batter, we projected the 3D trajectory of the baseball onto a 2D plane. From this, we also did not find any kinks in the projected acceleration of the baseball, which is analytically expected since there is no time dependence on the projections that were implemented. The next approach taken was to implement a machine learning program that would act as a batter, meaning that it would predict the trajectory of a baseball given some initial conditions and sparse updates along the actual trajectory of the ball. This approach ultimately did not reach fruition, but it was hoped that the machine would essentially be "tricked" in the same way that a human batter is by a curveball, implying that the effect of a sharp break is only an illusion caused by the discrete nature of tracking a pitch paired with the short processing time available to a batter.
